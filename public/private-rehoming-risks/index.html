The Risks of Rehoming a Dog Privately in South Africa

When you need to find a new home for your dog, posting an ad on Gumtree or a community Facebook group can feel like the quickest option. We understand why people do it — but we also know what can go wrong.

This page is not here to judge you. It's here to help you make a safer choice.

What happens when you post your dog online

When you list a dog as "free to a good home" or for a small rehoming fee on a public platform, you lose control of who responds. You might get dozens of messages within hours — some from genuine families, some from people you wouldn't want anywhere near your dog.

The problem is that the people you want to reach — responsible, caring adopters — are usually the ones who go through rescues. Public platforms can also attract people whose intentions are not always clear or safe.

Who watches these platforms

Dog fighting rings

Public “free to a good home” posts can be seen and shared by anyone. That makes it difficult to verify where a dog will go and creates a risk that the dog could be sought for harmful purposes, including illegal fighting.

Backyard breeders and puppy mills

Unsterilised dogs — especially popular purebreds and small breeds — may attract people looking to breed for profit. A friendly message is not proof of safe housing, veterinary care, or responsible breeding.

Flip-for-profit resellers

Free or low-cost dogs can also be collected for resale. If identity, address, ownership intent, and long-term care are not checked, a dog may be passed on again without your knowledge.

Warning signs to watch for

If you do choose to rehome privately, these are the behaviours that should make you pause:

  • They're in a hurry. "I can collect today." "I'll take the dog right now, no questions asked." Genuine adopters want to meet the dog and understand their needs before committing.
  • They don't ask about the dog. No questions about health, temperament, whether the dog is good with children, or why you're rehoming. A lack of questions gives you little evidence that the home is suitable.
  • They send someone else to collect. "My cousin will pick the dog up." You have no idea who is actually taking your dog or where they're going.
  • They refuse a home check or won't share their address. If someone will not let you verify where your dog will live, you cannot properly assess the home.
  • They offer more money than you asked for. Treat this as a reason to slow down and verify their identity and intentions carefully.

If you still decide to rehome privately

A rescue pathway is safer, but if you do go ahead with a private rehoming, a few minimum steps reduce your risk:

  • Meet in person before handing your dog over — never arrange a collection-only handoff with someone you haven't met.
  • Verify identity and address. Ask for ID and confirm where the dog will actually live.
  • Never let a third party collect the dog on behalf of someone you've been messaging.
  • Trust a slow, questioning adopter over a fast, uncritical one. Genuine adopters ask about the dog; they don't rush the handover.

A safer way to rehome

You don't have to navigate this alone. A relevant breed-specific rescue organisation may have processes to help identify warning signs and guide a safer placement.

Established rescues can explain their screening and placement process and help you avoid relying on the first person who answers a public post. Processes, capacity, fees, and outcomes vary, so confirm the details with the rescue you contact.
